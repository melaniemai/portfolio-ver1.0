Reflection

Reflection

Living in an area in Southwest Ohio with little to no resources about software development and tech made my academic journey to CS very interesting. When I was a teenager, I took interest in web development and began my "coding" journey as a self-taught learner. By the time college applications came around, my interest in coding had grew immensely. Everyone around me suggested I take up Computer Science as my major. I didn't get into the Computer Science program when I first transferred to UC. I ended up being in their Exploratory program and though I was disappointed, I didn't let it deter me. I went through the required engineering classes, practiced my coding skills in my free time, and even tried to get online certifications. I eventually made it into the CS program and the rest is history. I've taken interesting CS classes such as Data Structures, Python Programming, and Discrete Structures. I've met some incredible people along my academic journey and it doesn't stop there. UC's Co-op program for Engineers has allowed me to get hands-on experience in the professional tech industry. I've been able to apply my coding skills to the projects I've done at Siemens while gaining new knowledge from my coworkers. I also had an amazing mentor at Siemens who really let me grow personally and professionally.

My time at Siemens has really impacted my journey as a CS student. I already had interest in full-stack development but the team I was on at Siemens dealt with Meshing and Geometric Abstraction. Coming into my co-op I didn't know what meshing and abstraction had to do with coding, nor did I know what meshing and abstraction was in general, but thanks to my co-op, I had the opportunity to be exposed to different career paths in CS. I've become more open-minded about my future and what I would like to do.

Although my co-op came with great and knowledgable experience, it also came with frustrations. During the first few weeks of my co-op, I felt very small and frustrated with myself. I was working on projects and felt as if my coding skills weren't up to par with everyone else at the company. I was setting high goals for myself that I couldn't reach. I wanted to be able to do projects on my own without the help of my mentor. I wanted to impress my coworkers and be independent. It was frustrating when I couldn't reach those goals as a student on her first co-op. I just wanted to do well, but I realized my version of "doing well" was going to be almost impossible since I've never been in a professional tech environment before, not to mention I was new to the company. I learned that being a "skilled" developer and a great team member takes time. It also takes throwing away my shyness and not being afraid to ask questions. It takes making mistakes and failures to truly allow myself to find the right path I should be on. If there's one thing I would do differently during my first co-op, it would be to stop worrying about not being the best developer or trying to impress everyone. I should focus on my skills and how to improve myself and not how to impress others.

I've completed two co-ops so far and my plan for the remaining co-ops I have is to truly find what career path I want to take prior to graduating, along with strengthening my hard and soft skills. I hope to create more complex projects to showcase my skills, and I really hope to have fun along the way.
